# Why Architects Should Treat AI Like a Contract, Not a Prompt

> **Reading time:** 6 minutes  
> **Category:** Workflow Guides

---

## Quick Answer (15-second read)

If your AI results change wildly from day to day, the problem is usually not the tool — it’s the brief.

Instead of writing “a better prompt”, write a **small contract**:
- what you will provide (inputs)
- what the AI must return (outputs)
- what must not change (constraints)
- where you will review and decide (checkpoints)

This makes your workflow repeatable — like a real studio process.

---

## What Goes Wrong With “Prompt-Only” Work

Most architects start like this:
- “Make this render more premium”
- “Make it more Japanese minimal”
- “Try a warmer afternoon light”

These can work once, but they drift over time because:
- the reference set changes
- the team changes
- the project stage changes
- you forget which choices were “locked”

The result: one-off wins, but no reliable pipeline.

---

## A Simple Contract Structure (Architect-Friendly)

Think of this as a one-page agreement between you and the AI.

### 1) Inputs (what you give)
- Base image(s): massing / clay render / photo
- Context: location, climate, usage
- References: 3–8 images max (not a moodboard dump)
- View list: exterior hero / courtyard / lobby, etc.

### 2) Outputs (what you want back)
- 3 variations, each with a distinct direction
- 1 “safe” option and 2 “stretch” options
- captions: one sentence per image, describing intent

### 3) Constraints (what cannot change)
- massing stays the same
- camera angle stays the same
- keep the program cues readable (entrance, circulation)
- no fake signage, no random people in the foreground

### 4) Review checkpoints (where humans decide)
- **Checkpoint A:** pick 1 direction (do not iterate all 3)
- **Checkpoint B:** lock materials + lighting
- **Checkpoint C:** only detail refinement after lock

---

## The “Two Locks” Rule (Stops Endless Iteration)

In practice, two locks prevent 80% of wasted cycles:

1) **Lock the camera** (view + crop + horizon)
2) **Lock the material direction** (primary material + secondary)

Once those are locked, iteration becomes productive instead of chaotic.

## Key Takeaways

- Prompts win moments; contracts win projects.
- Inputs + outputs + constraints are more important than “clever wording”.
- Lock camera and material direction early to stop the “one more try” loop.

## FAQ

**Q: Is this only for big firms?**  
A: No. Small studios benefit more, because you have less time to waste.

**Q: Won’t this make the work less creative?**  
A: It reduces chaos, not creativity. You still explore — you just explore on purpose.

**Q: Where does this fit in a real workflow?**  
A: Right after you choose a massing/view. Before you do heavy refinement.
