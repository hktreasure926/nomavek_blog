## Quick Answer

Most AI image tools understand the current input: your prompt and your reference image.

They do not reliably remember the full project: the brief, constraints, rejected options, or what the studio decided last week.

Project understanding needs a workflow outside the image tool: a locked brief, a decision log, and a board that becomes the working memory.

---

## The Real Problem: Re-Explaining The Project Every Time

Small studios lose time when the AI output looks good but drifts away from the project intent.

The next image ignores the same key constraint again: budget, height, frontage, daylight, planning, or client taste.

The team ends up prompting instead of deciding.

This is not because the architect is unclear. It is because many tools are optimized for one-shot output, not project continuity.

---

## Image Tools vs Project-Aware Systems

### 1. Image tools: fast options

Tools like LookX and Archicad's AI Visualizer help turn prompts, sketches, or model context into visual alternatives quickly.

That is useful for mood exploration, massing/style alternatives, and early client conversations.

But image tools are weaker at remembering what the studio already decided, carrying a stable brief from week to week, or building a client-ready explanation.

### 2. Agent and tool workflows: project continuity

Agent-style systems can use tools, read files, and work from a memory layer.

The shift is simple: the project should live in documents and boards, not inside a single prompt.

---

## A Simple Project Memory Setup For Small Studios

### Step 1: One locked brief

- Project type, site, and scale
- Client priorities, ranked
- Hard constraints: planning, area, height, budget, timeline
- Must-keep items: views, trees, access, facade logic
- Must-avoid items: client dislikes, banned references, wrong material tone

### Step 2: A short decision log

- What the client reacted to
- What was rejected
- What the team decided to develop next

### Step 3: One board per round

Instead of sending a folder of images, produce a board with a brief summary, two to four options, a short rationale, risks or unknowns, and next actions.

That board becomes the memory the AI can follow later.

---

## Why This Matters For Client Trust

Clients do not buy more images.

They buy a clearer direction, a credible explanation, and a plan for what happens next.

If AI makes ten images but the studio cannot explain why one is stronger, confidence drops.

## FAQ

### Can't I just write a better prompt?

A better prompt helps the first image.

The real challenge is the second week, the third round, and the version history.

That is why memory needs to live outside the prompt.

### Does BIM-based AI solve project understanding?

Not automatically.

BIM contains information, but the workflow still needs a human-readable brief and decision record, or the reason behind each option gets lost.

### What is the smallest version that works?

- One locked brief page
- One decision log
- One board per round
